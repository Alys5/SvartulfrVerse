## Overview

The Undergraduate Students Association Council (USAC) is the official student government of UCLA. It serves as the primary representative body for undergraduate students, managing a multimillion-dollar budget, funding student institutions, and advocating for student interests within the university administration.

USAC is one of the most well-funded undergraduate student governments in the United States, which gives it unusual institutional weight. Its decisions affect campus policy, event programming, and the allocation of resources across more than 1,000 registered student institutions.

**Mission:** Represent undergraduate student interests, allocate student fees, fund institutions, and advocate for student needs within the UC system governance structure.

---

## Structure

### Executive Officers

The USAC executive branch consists of elected officers who serve one-year terms:
- **President** — Chief executive. Sets agenda, represents students to administration, oversees USAC operations.
- **Vice President(s)** — Support the president, manage specific portfolios (external affairs, internal affairs, campus events).
- **General Representative(s)** — Elected at-large to represent the broader student body on specific issues.

### Legislative Council

The legislative branch debates and votes on resolutions, budget allocations, and policy positions. Council members represent specific constituencies (academic departments, identity groups, geographic areas). The council is where most political maneuvering occurs — coalition building, bloc voting, and public debate.

### Judicial Branch

The USAC Judicial Board interprets the USAC constitution, adjudicates election disputes, and reviews constitutional challenges. It functions as a check on executive and legislative power within the student government system.

### Committees

Standing committees manage specific functional areas:
- **Cultural Affairs** — Funding and support for cultural institutions and events.
- **Academic Affairs** — Advocacy on curriculum, grading policy, academic resources.
- **Student Wellness** — Mental health, food security, housing advocacy.
- **Sustainability** — Environmental policy and campus sustainability initiatives.
- **Community Service** — Volunteer coordination and service programming.

## Election System

### Election Cycle

USAC elections occur annually in the spring quarter. Campaigning begins weeks before the vote, with candidates and slates competing for visibility across campus.

### Campaigning

Candidates campaign through social media, campus tabling, flyering, debate performances, and endorsements from student institutions. Campaigns are low-budget by professional political standards but high-energy — candidates rely on personal networks, institutional endorsements, and social media reach.

### Voter Participation

Turnout is typically low (single-digit to low double-digit percentages of the undergraduate population), which means organized blocs and motivated minorities can determine outcomes. This dynamic makes USAC elections a microcosm of real political systems: the organized few outweigh the disengaged many.

### Political Blocs

Candidates often run as part of slates or coalitions organized around shared platforms: identity-based blocs, Greek Life blocs, activist blocs, and independent candidates. Bloc discipline and cross-bloc alliances determine election outcomes.

### Controversies

Common USAC election controversies include:
- **Vote manipulation allegations** — claims of bloc voting, ballot stuffing, or digital voting irregularities.
- **Identity politics debates** — disputes over representation, platform priorities, and which communities deserve funding.
- **Administration interference** — perceptions that the university administration favors certain candidates or slates.
- **Budget allocation fights** — post-election disputes over which institutions receive funding and how much.

## Areas of Influence

### Student Organizations

USAC controls funding allocation for registered student institutions. This gives USAC significant indirect influence over which institutions thrive, which events happen, and which communities receive institutional support. Organizations must apply for USAC funding, creating a dependency relationship.

### Campus Events

USAC funds and co-sponsors major campus events — concerts, cultural celebrations, awareness campaigns, and end-of-year programming. Event funding is a visible and politically sensitive function.

### Student Services

USAC advocates for student services: mental health resources, food pantry, housing assistance, transportation subsidies, and academic support. These advocacy efforts connect student government to tangible student needs.

### University Advocacy

USAC represents undergraduate interests in discussions with the UCLA administration and the UC Office of the President. Issues include tuition, campus safety, diversity initiatives, and academic policy.

### Campus Policy Discussions

USAC takes positions on campus policy debates: free speech, campus policing, sustainability mandates, and institutional responses to current events. These positions generate public debate and media attention.

## Narrative Utility

### Political Intrigue

USAC is the most narratively productive political environment at UCLA. Elections, budget votes, and policy debates create structured conflict with clear stakes, visible opponents, and public consequences. For characters involved in student government, every meeting is a potential power play.

### Election Campaigns

USAC election cycles provide annual narrative anchors. Characters can be candidates, campaign managers, endorsers, or kingmakers. Campaign storylines offer: public debates, social media warfare, endorsement negotiations, scandal management, and the tension between personal conviction and political pragmatism.

### Faction Building

USAC's coalition structure mirrors larger political systems. Characters build alliances across institutions, identity groups, and interest blocs. The skills practiced in USAC politics — negotiation, public persuasion, coalition management — translate directly to corporate and family power dynamics.

### Public Debates

USAC council meetings are public forums where contentious issues are debated. Characters can use these forums to make political statements, challenge opponents, or advance policy agendas. Public debate creates visibility and accountability.

### Scandals

Student government scandals — funding misuse, election fraud, personal misconduct, leaked communications — provide high-drama, low-stakes conflict. Scandals can destroy reputations, end political careers, and create lasting enmities.

### Coalition Politics

The need to build coalitions across diverse interests creates complex political dynamics. Characters must negotiate with groups they disagree with, make compromises that alienate their base, and manage the tension between principle and pragmatism.

### Leadership Rivalries

USAC officer positions are visible and competitive. Rivalries between candidates, between officers with different visions, and between the executive and legislative branches create ongoing interpersonal conflict.
