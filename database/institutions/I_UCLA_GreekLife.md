## Scope

UCLA Greek Life is the fraternity and sorority ecosystem operating within UCLA. It functions as a parallel social infrastructure to the university's academic system — providing housing, social programming, alumni networks, and status hierarchies that persist long after graduation.

For SvartúlfrVerse, Greek Life matters because it is the primary social organizing system for UCLA students outside of academics and athletics. It shapes who students live with, who they socialize with, which events they attend, and which networks they inherit. For characters from powerful families, Greek Life is also an arena where family status, expectation, and rebellion play out in microcosm.

This record documents the system itself, not individual chapters. For a specific fraternity, see [I_KappaSigmaAlpha.md](../institutions/I_KappaSigmaAlpha.md).

## Organizational Structure

UCLA's Greek system is organized under several governing councils:

### Interfraternity Council (IFC)

The IFC governs most fraternities at UCLA. IFC fraternities are typically social fraternities — focused on brotherhood, social events, alumni networking, and campus involvement. They recruit primarily during fall and spring rush periods. Each chapter maintains a chapter house (or designated residential facility) and operates under both university and national alumni council oversight.

### National Panhellenic Conference (NPC)

The NPC governs sororities at UCLA. Panhellenic sororities follow a formal recruitment process (primary rush in fall, with structured rounds of house visits). Like IFC fraternities, they maintain chapter houses and operate under national organizational authority. Sororities provide the same social infrastructure as fraternities — housing, networking, philanthropy, social events — with separate but parallel governance.

### National Pan-Hellenic Council (NPHC)

The NPHC (the "Divine Nine") governs historically Black fraternities and sororities. These organizations have distinct histories, traditions, and cultural significance rooted in Black collegiate experience. They operate under separate recruitment and governance processes from IFC and NPC organizations.

### Multicultural Greek Council (MGC)

The MGC governs culturally-based fraternities and sororities — Latino, Asian Pacific Islander, multicultural, and other identity-affiliated organizations. These chapters provide social infrastructure for students seeking Greek Life within culturally specific frameworks.

## Social Influence

### Social Events

Greek Life drives a significant portion of UCLA's social event calendar. Formals, semi-formals, mixers, themed parties, and philanthropy events are organized by chapters and hosted at chapter houses, rented venues, or outdoor spaces. These events are the primary social anchors for Greek-affiliated students and attract non-affiliated students as guests.

For SvartúlfrVerse, Greek events provide natural social settings: crowded, alcohol-adjacent, status-conscious, and structured around in-group/out-group dynamics.

### Networking

Greek alumni networks are one of the system's primary outputs. Alumni maintain connections with active chapters, provide mentorship, fund scholarships, and offer professional networking. In Los Angeles, where industry connections determine career trajectories, Greek alumni networks provide access that complements (or rivals) family corporate networks.

For characters from wealthy families, the question is whether Greek networking supplements family power or competes with it.

### Status Systems

Greek Life creates an internal status hierarchy that operates independently of (but interacts with) broader social status. Chapter prestige, individual position within a chapter, and which events a student is invited to all function as social currency. This micro-hierarchy mirrors larger power dynamics and provides narrative-ready conflict.

### Inter-Chapter Rivalries

Competition between chapters — for campus influence, recruitment numbers, philanthropy dollars, and social prestige — creates structural rivalry independent of individual personality conflicts. These rivalries escalate during recruitment seasons and peak at campus-wide events.

### Recruitment Culture

Rush (formal recruitment) is the annual cycle that determines chapter membership. It is a high-stakes, high-anxiety process for both recruiters and recruits. Chapters evaluate potential members; potential members evaluate chapters. Legacy status (having family members in a chapter) provides advantage but does not guarantee membership.

For family-oriented narratives, recruitment is where generational expectations become explicit: Will the legacy join? Will they be accepted? Will they refuse?

### Chapter Housing

Greek chapter houses are residential facilities owned or leased by national organizations and operated by active members. They function as both homes and social venues — the combination creates a unique social environment where private life and public performance overlap. Chapter houses are where members live, host events, hold meetings, and form the closest bonds.

For narrative purposes, chapter houses provide enclosed social environments with built-in power dynamics: who lives where, who controls common spaces, who is invited inside, who is excluded.

## Narrative Utility

### Rivalries

Inter-chapter rivalries, recruitment competition, and house-versus-house dynamics provide ready-made conflict structures. These can be personal (two characters from rival houses) or systemic (chapters competing for influence).

### Social Drama

Greek Life's combination of close living quarters, alcohol, status competition, and social events creates structurally high-drama environments. Relationship drama, friendship betrayal, and social climbing are all natural within this framework.

### Recruitment as Story Engine

Rush cycles provide annual narrative anchors. Characters navigating recruitment face questions of loyalty, identity, and belonging. Legacy characters face family expectations. Non-legacy characters face questions of access and authenticity.

### Alliances

Cross-chapter alliances — partnerships for events, philanthropy, or political influence — create broader network dynamics. Characters can leverage Greek alliances for campus influence, information access, or social protection.

### Leadership Struggles

Chapter officer elections, policy decisions, and internal disciplinary processes provide micro-level governance conflict. Characters in leadership positions must balance chapter interests, national organization expectations, university rules, and personal relationships.

### Alumni Influence

Active alumni involvement in chapter affairs creates intergenerational dynamics. Alumni can be mentors, critics, or enforcers of tradition. For wealthy families, alumni who hold corporate or political power can use Greek connections to exert influence over current members.

### Family Traditions

When multiple family members belong to the same chapter, Greek Life becomes a vehicle for family dynamics. Expectation, legacy pressure, sibling comparison, and generational conflict all map naturally onto fraternity/sorority membership. Jasper's refusal to join KSA (see [I_KappaSigmaAlpha.md](../institutions/I_KappaSigmaAlpha.md)) is a prime example.
